Chapter I

The Letter Smelled of Dragon Smoke

On the morning of Ketan's eleventh birthday, three things happened: he woke up, he spilled his mango juice, and an enormous tawny owl crashed through his bedroom window with a letter clenched in its talons. The envelope was sealed in dark red wax stamped with a crest of four animals — a lion, an eagle, a badger, and a serpent — and it smelled, very distinctly, of dragon smoke.

His mother read it first, then his father, then both of them together, in silence. Then they handed it to Ketan.

"Dear Mr. Ketan,
We are pleased to inform you that you have been accepted to Hogwarts School of Witchcraft and Wizardry.
The term begins on 1st September. We await your owl by no later than 31st July.

Yours sincerely,
Professor M. McGonagall, Deputy Headmistress"

At the bottom, scrawled in a different ink — slightly orange and faintly smoking — was a handwritten note: "P.S. — You will need extra dragonhide gloves. Just trust us."

Ketan blinked. Then he grinned the widest grin of his life.

Chapter II

The Sorting Hat Sings of Fire

The Great Hall of Hogwarts was more magnificent than any palace Ketan had ever imagined. Thousands of candles floated above four long tables where hundreds of students in black robes chattered and laughed. The ceiling was bewitched to look like the night sky — deep indigo, scattered with real-looking stars.

Ketan stood in a nervous line of first-years, craning his neck. Beside him, a boy with spectacles and a thick spellbook tucked under one arm introduced himself quietly: "Karthik. Ravenclaw, probably."

A round-faced boy behind them smiled broadly. "Kalai," he whispered. "I've memorised forty-seven potion recipes already. Just in case."

Then from the other side of the line came a cool, confident voice: "Aadhini. Don't be nervous — the hat doesn't bite. I asked it." She paused. "It was a very short conversation."

One by one, the hat sang their futures. Karthik to Ravenclaw. Kalai to Hufflepuff. Aadhini, after a long dramatic pause, to Slytherin. And Ketan — the hat barely touched his head before shouting:

"GRYFFINDOR!"

The four friends — sorted into four different houses — locked eyes across the Great Hall and, without saying a word, all understood something: they were going to cause a great deal of trouble together.

Chapter III

The Dragon Beneath the Lake

Nobody expected the first Hogwarts emergency to arrive on a Tuesday. Ketan was returning from Astronomy class when he heard it — a thunderous splash from the direction of the Black Lake, followed by a column of orange fire shooting fifty feet into the night sky.

He ran. So did Karthik, who happened to be studying on the lakeside bench, and Kalai, who was collecting rare water-weeds for a potion. Aadhini arrived last, already pulling on her dragonhide gloves — the extra pair Professor McGonagall had quietly handed her on day one.

"That," said Karthik, adjusting his spectacles and staring at the enormous reptilian creature thrashing in the shallows, "is a Hebridean Black. Welsh dragons are green. Norwegian Ridgebacks have spines. But the Hebridean is—"

"On fire and very upset," Kalai finished. "Yes, I can see that."

The dragon roared again. Students screamed from the castle windows above. Professor Hagrid's giant silhouette came crashing through the trees, but he was still half a forest away. And the dragon — Ketan could now see — had one enormous claw tangled in what appeared to be a net of enchanted steel cables.

"It's caught," said Ketan quietly. "It's not attacking. It's scared."

He took a step forward. His three friends grabbed his arm.

"Ketan," Aadhini said carefully, "that animal breathes fire."

"I know," said Ketan. "But so would you, if you were terrified and trapped."

He kept walking.

Chapter IV

Four Wands, One Dragon

Ketan stood ten feet from the dragon. It was the size of a city bus and twice as loud, thrashing its enormous tail and sending jets of flame skyward. The steel cables — some kind of poacher's trap — were cutting into its left hind leg. Every movement made it worse.

Behind him, he heard three sets of footsteps on the gravel. His friends had followed.

"Right," said Karthik, opening his spellbook mid-stride. "According to Fantastical Beasts — Volume Three — the calming signal for a Hebridean Black is a low humming vibration at exactly the pitch of a—"

"Karthik," said Kalai, uncorking a small bottle, "I have a Calming Draught. I made it yesterday. Can I throw it?"

"Aim for the nose," said Aadhini. She stepped past all of them, looked at the dragon, and did something extraordinary. She began to speak — not in English, not in any language Ketan recognised — a soft, sibilant hissing that rose and fell like wind through reeds. Parseltongue. The dragon went still.

It turned one enormous eye toward Aadhini. The fire in its nostrils dimmed to a glow.

"Now," she said quietly. "Cut the cables. All three of you — now."

Ketan raised his wand: "Liberare!"
Karthik cast: "Solvaro!"
Kalai flung his draught — it shattered perfectly against the dragon's snout — and added: "Tranquillus!"

The cables snapped. The dragon let out a low, thunderous sound — not a roar. Something softer. Something that, if Ketan had to name it, sounded rather like a very large cat purring.

It lowered its enormous head to the ground and looked at them — all four of them — with those burning amber eyes. Then, slowly, it spread its wings, pushed off the surface of the lake, and rose into the Scottish sky.

They watched it until it was a speck among the stars.

"Right," said Karthik eventually. "Fifty points to every house, I'd imagine."

"A hundred," said Aadhini, examining her gloves. "At least."

Kalai sat down on a rock. "I need to make more Calming Draught."

Ketan said nothing. He was still watching the sky.

Chapter V — Epilogue

The Dragon Comes Back

The next morning brought two things: a detention note from Professor McGonagall for being out after hours — and a unanimous award of one hundred and twenty points to each of the four houses, the highest ever given on a single night in Hogwarts history. Professor Dumbledore read the announcement at breakfast with a twinkle in his eye that suggested he was not entirely surprised.

But the real surprise came at noon.

They were at lunch when every window in the Great Hall suddenly filled with shadow. Students screamed, then gasped, then fell completely silent — because there, hovering outside the enchanted ceiling, was a Hebridean Black dragon, its amber eyes scanning the hall below. In one claw it carried something: a single scale, dark green and edged with copper, the size of a dinner plate.

It found Ketan. Across hundreds of upturned faces, across house tables and floating candles and enchanted ceiling, the dragon held his gaze. Then it opened its enormous jaws — and instead of fire, it breathed out a warm cloud of golden mist that drifted through the windows and smelled of woodsmoke and mountain wind.

The scale fell through an open window and landed, with a gentle thunk, precisely in front of Ketan.

Then the dragon was gone.

The hall erupted. Students climbed on chairs. Teachers stood from their seats. Professor Hagrid was openly weeping into his enormous handkerchief.

Karthik pushed his glasses up and said very calmly: "Dragon scales are extremely rare. According to Appendix D of Ollivander's Wand Components—"

"He said thank you," Aadhini interrupted softly, still looking at the empty window. "That's what the mist means. In Draconic. He said thank you."

Kalai was already writing a letter to his parents. "They're never going to believe this."

Ketan held the scale. It was warm in his hands, warm as sunlight, warm as the first morning of the best summer of your life. He thought of the dragon rising into the dark sky. He thought of four different house robes, four different wands, one terrified animal, and one shared decision.

He set the scale on the table and smiled.

This was only the first year.
There were six more to go.
